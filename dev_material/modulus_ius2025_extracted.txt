ModulUS: A Sandbox for High-Resolution
Wearable Ultrasound Development
Christoph Leitner ∗, Marco Giordano ∗, Martin Tanner ∗, Federico Villani ∗, Michele Magno ∗,
Luca Benini ∗†,
∗Department of Information Technology and Electrical Engineering, ETH Zurich, Switzerland
†DEI, University of Bologna, Bologna, Italy
Abstract—We introduce ModulUS, a modular sandbox plat-
form for wearable ultrasound (US) development. The four-
board system integrates a 32-channel pulser, an analog frontend
(AFE) with bandwidth reduction, and an ARM Cortex-M4
microcontroller (MCU) with a dual 5 Msps, 12 bitanalog-digital
converter (ADC), each implemented as a swappable module to
support rapid prototyping and evaluation of alternative circuit
architectures. To demonstrate its functionality, we validated the
AFE board, which reduces the effective signal bandwidth by
a factor of 4 while preserving structural features, with envelope
fidelity confirmed against digital Hilbert transforms (R 2 = 0.88).
Compared to State-of-the-Art (SoA) platforms, ModulUS-derived
designs set a new benchmark in power efficiency, achieving
the lowest reported average power normalized by excitation
frequency (0.6 mW/MHz), down to two orders of magnitude
below existing MCU- and Field Programmable Gate Arrays
(FPGA)-based systems. By shifting bandwidth reduction (BWR)
into the analog domain, ModulUS enables high-frequency, low-
power US acquisition and establishes a flexible, reconfigurable
testbed for next-generation wearable US technologies.
Index Terms—Wearable Ultrasound, Modular Design, Band-
width Reduction, Analog Front-End, Low-Power Ultrasound,
Microcontroller-Based, High-Frequency Ultrasound
I. I NTRODUCTION
A growing trend in wearable US system design is the shift
from conventional medical imaging towards the acquisition,
processing, and machine interpretation of raw US data directly
on digital probes [1]. Emerging non-imaging US wearables are
typically optimized for recording A-mode or M-mode echoes,
often via distributed transducer networks [2], and leverage ma-
chine learning (ML) for data interpretation, thereby reducing
the traditionally high operator dependence of US.
From a system architecture perspective, two main templates
(see Tab. I) dominate the field: (a) medium-channel-count
wearable platforms with up to 32 channels implemented on
FPGA-based architectures, and (b) low-channel-count plat-
forms (1–8 channels, often partially time-multiplexed) employ-
ing MCU-based topologies. The primary differences between
these architectures lie in their achievable axial spatial reso-
lutions (i.e., the maximum transducer excitation frequencies),
temporal resolutions (i.e., the maximum pulse-repetition fre-
quencies), number of parallel receive (Rx) channels, and trade-
offs in power consumption and form factor (i.e., volume).
FPGA-based wearables [5] are, in principle, fully capable
US systems that can support standard medical modalities such
as A- and M-mode scans, B-mode imaging, and Doppler,

For many MCU-based US wearables without imaging ca-
pabilities, hardware constraints make it impractical or even
impossible to capture modalities in which phase information
is critical (e.g., beamforming, fast displacement tracking,
or Doppler) [8]. Even when radio frequency (RF) data are
recorded and digitized, phase information is often discarded
during subsequent digital signal processing [9], [10]. Sup-
porting phase information typically requires full-bandwidth
ADC, which imposes substantial computational overhead, of-
ten exceeding 5× that of bandwidth reduced methods [11]. To
address this limitation, analog signal-conditioning approaches
for wearable US have been proposed [12], which reduce the
effective bandwidth of the received signals before digitization
while preserving echo resolution that is set by the transmit
signal bandwidth. By easing the digitization burden, these
methods can expand the capabilities of MCU-based wearable
US systems, relaxing existing hardware requirements and
enabling higher-frequency, higher-resolution applications.

In this context, we present ModulUS, a modular and flexible
hardware sandbox for wearable US development. The primary
contribution of this work is a detailed description of the
platform’s design concept and a demonstration of its func-
tionality, showing how it employs an AFE processing board
to preserve high axial resolution with a 10 MHz transducer
while reducing conversion, sequencing, and digital computing
requirements. This design enables data acquisition without
complex high-speed interconnects to ADCs (e.g., LVDS or
JESD204), instead leveraging an ARM Cortex-M4 MCU with
advanced analog features and an integrated 5 Msps ADC.
II. S YSTEM DESIGN
A. Architecture
The ModulUS system architecture (Fig. 1) is designed for
modularity, allowing flexible modification of the core building
blocks of a wearable US platform. These include the Tx and
Rx stages, the AFE, and the control and compute unit. The
complete system (Fig. 2) consists of four functional boards
and measures 260 x 115 x 40 mm:
1) The TxRx board integrates a 32-channel US pulser
(STHVUP32, STMicroelectronics, Switzerland), with 8
exposed channels in Tx and Rx,
2) The AFE board integrates the BWR circuits for all chan-
nels, as described in [11], enabling envelope detection,
3) The control and compute board is based on an
STM32L496 MCU (STMicroelectronics, Switzerland),
featuring a dual 5 Msps, 12 bit on-chip ADC. It provides
a USB-C interface for data transfer either via Universal
Asynchronous Receiver/Transmitter (UART) or through
an NRF53 Bluetooth low energy (BLE)-capable MCU
to a connected subsystem,
4) The ModulUS motherboard interconnects all daughter
modules using high-density board-to-board connectors.
B. Functionality
The pulser is configured by the MCU via a Quad Serial
Peripheral Interface (QSPI) interface, which programs its se-
quencing logic. After boot, the MCU initializes the pulser and
starts pulse generation, transmitting signals to the transducer.
The returning echoes pass through the transmit-receive switch
(TR) to the AFE board, where envelope detection is applied.
For each received US pulse, an interrupt triggers an ADC
capture on the MCU. The acquired data is then processed,
stored locally, or transmitted to the host system. Power is
supplied to each board via dedicated edge connectors, ensuring
electrical isolation between subsystems and enabling flexible
reconfiguration for experimental setups.

C. Figure of Merit Comparison
To compare wearable US systems with different transducer
center frequencies ( f TX), we define the average power per
Rx channel normalized by f TX (mW/MHz) as a figure of
merit. Since higher frequencies improve axial resolution but
proportionally increase the energy required for signal conver-
sion, storage, computation, and transfer [15], normalization
provides a resolution-aware basis for comparing MCU- and
FPGA-based architectures with the results presented in Table
I and discussed in Section IV.
III. E XPERIMENTAL TESTING
A. Setup
The system was validated using a single-channel 10 MHz
transducer (Piezohannas, China) immersed in water. The trans-
ducer emitted five-cycle pulses ( n = 5) at 15 Vpp toward a
0.7 mm polyethylene terephthalate (PET) disc, which served
as a reflecting interface. Both raw Rx echoes and processed
analog envelopes, generated by the AFE module, were ac-
quired simultaneously on an acquisition card (ADQ412, Tele-
dyn Technologies, USA).
Axial resolution was approximated as half the spatial pulse
length (SPL) [16], with SPL = nλ. Using a sound speed of
c = 1540 m s−1 and a center frequency of f = 10 MHz, the
wavelength is λ = c/f = 0 .154 mm. This yields SPL =
0.77 mm for n = 5 cycles and an axial resolution of about
0.38 mm, sufficient to resolve the two surfaces (upper and
lower boundaries) of the 0.7 mm disc.

C. Results
The mean frequency of the raw Rx signals was approxi-
mately 2.9 MHz, while the BWR envelopes exhibited a mean
frequency of 0.7 MHz, corresponding to a reduction by a
factor of about four. The PET disc produced two resolv-
able echo peaks in the BWR envelopes, demonstrating that
essential structural information was preserved despite the
reduced bandwidth. Moreover, analog and digitally derived
envelopes showed close correspondence with R2 = 0 .88,
with differences limited to the expected phase removal. The
approximately fourfold bandwidth reduction enabled direct
digitization using the on-chip ADC of the MCU, eliminat-
ing the need for high-speed FPGA-based acquisition. Fig. 3
illustrates representative results, showing the raw Rx signal,
the analog envelope obtained from the AFE, and the digitally
derived envelope for direct comparison.
IV. D ISCUSSION
The results confirm that ModulUS achieves sub-millimeter
axial resolution while operating entirely on a low-power MCU.
This capability is enabled by the analog BWR frontend,
which compresses the effective signal spectrum by a factor of
four without erasing structural information. When positioned
against the SoA (Tab. I), platforms based on the ModulUS
concept (e.g., PuLsE [6]) show a distinct trade-off profile.
FPGA-based systems such as TinyProbe [5] offer superior
flexibility, with up to 32 channels and native support for

imaging and advanced US modalities (e.g., Doppler). How-
ever, supporting multiple channels with high spatio-temporal
resolutions comes at the cost of increased power consump-
tion ( 970 mW) and a substantially larger volume ( 39.9 cm3)
compared to MCU-based single channel systems. In contrast,
MCU-based solutions such as WULPUS [3], USoP [4], and
PuLsE [6] achieve much lower power and volume but remain
fundamentally limited to single-channel operation.
To enable a fair comparison across architectures operating
at different transducer frequencies, we introduced the normal-
ized average power per Rx channel ( mW/MHz) as a figure
of merit (see Section II-C). Using this metric, ModulUS-
derived systems such as PuLsE [6] set a new benchmark
with 0.6 mW/MHz, outperforming all previously reported
wearable designs (Tab. I). For reference, USoP consumes
102.3 mW/MHz, TinyProbe 2.0 mW/MHz, and WULPUS
5.5 mW/MHz. This efficiency gain, exceeding two orders of
magnitude compared to USoP, and remaining 3–10x lower
than TinyProbe and WULPUS, demonstrates that envelope-
domain acquisition with analog BWR can decouple high-
resolution imaging from excessive energy budgets.
These results have important implications. First, Modu-
lUS demonstrates that MCU-based platforms are not strictly
limited to low-frequency or low-resolution applications. By
pushing BWR into the analog domain, ModulUS enables
operation of 10 MHz transducers with power figures that sur-
pass other ultra-low-power MCU solutions working at much
lower frequencies. Second, the modular design of ModulUS
opens up a level of reconfigurability for the development of
individual wearable system designs. Unlike highly integrated
devices, ModulUS exposes the AFE, pulser, and control logic
as interchangeable components, lowering the barrier for future
research in alternative BWR schemes or wearable transducer
development.
Naturally, these gains come with limitations. ModulUS
processes only one channel at a time; this rules out simultane-
ous multi-channel imaging. Furthermore, the envelope-domain
path discards phase information, meaning it cannot directly
support applications such as Doppler or displacement tracking.
Finally, ModulUS is not a wearable US system per se but
a catalyst. Its modularity and flexibility come at the cost of
size and weight, positioning it as a sandbox for wearable US
development rather than a deployable device.
V. C ONCLUSION
We introduced ModulUS, a four-board sandbox for de-
veloping high-resolution wearable US systems. By shifting
BWR into the analog domain, ModulUS moves the per-
formance bottleneck away from digital acquisition, enabling
sub-millimeter resolution on low-power MCU platforms. The
system preserves structural information at 10 MHz while re-
ducing bandwidth more than fourfold. Its modular design,
exposing pulser, AFE, as well as control and compute units
as interchangeable modules, provides a versatile platform for
wearable US research. These gains entail trade-offs. Com-
pared to higher-power FPGA-based systems, ModulUS is
limited to single-channel operation, restricting simultaneous
2-D imaging. Furthermore, its envelope-based BWR omits
phase information, required by many modalities such as delay-
and-sum beamforming, Doppler, or displacement tracking.
Addressing this would involve phase-preserving strategies
(e.g., I/Q sampling or alternative BWR methods), at the cost
of higher power and system complexity. Future work will
focus on refining ModulUS through standardized module form
factors, comprehensive in vivo validation, and open-sourcing
the hardware design.
